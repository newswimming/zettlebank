In 1920, the garment industry was the single largest employer of Japanese women in Los Angeles. While the white-owned industrial "sweatshops" were located just a few blocks west in what is now the Fashion District, Little Tokyo (_Rafu_) developed its own internal garment economy. This system was built on a hierarchy of skill, from home-based "finishing" to professional tailoring.

### 1. The "Contract" Shop (Small-Scale Factories)

The most common "factory" job was in a small, Japanese-owned contract shop, often located in the basements or the second floors of brick buildings along **East First Street** and **Los Angeles Street**.

- **The Power Machine Operator:** This was the primary role. Women operated heavy, industrial-grade Singer sewing machines powered by a central overhead belt system. The work was "piece-rate," meaning they were paid per garment. A skilled operator in 1920 could produce dozens of shirts or pairs of work trousers a day.
    
- **The Specialty Operator:** Highly skilled women worked on "buttonhole machines" or "hemming machines." These required more precision and often paid a slightly higher piece-rate because a mistake could ruin a nearly finished garment.
    
- **The "Folder" and "Trimmer":** Entry-level roles, often filled by newly arrived picture brides. They didn't sew but spent 10–12 hours a day clipping loose threads with small snips (_trimmers_) and folding finished garments for shipment to the larger wholesalers.
    

### 2. The "Naishoku" (Home-Based Piecework)

For women living in SROs near Weller Street with small children, the "factory" came to them.

- **Hand-Finishing:** Larger shops would outsource the delicate work. Women would carry large bundles of semi-finished clothes back to their tiny hotel rooms. They performed "blind stitching" on hems, sewed on buttons by hand, or added intricate embroidery.
    
- **The Living Room Factory:** In 1920, it was common for a single SRO room to contain a sewing machine, a bed, and a kerosene stove. The woman would work while her children played on the floor amidst piles of denim or wool.
    

### 3. Professional Tailoring and "Western" Dressmaking

Because of the social pressure to appear "Americanized," there was a massive demand for Western-style clothing within the Nikkei community.

- **The Dressmaker’s Assistant:** Shops like the **Rafu Yossai Gakuen** (L.A. Sewing School) acted as both schools and factories. Women worked as apprentices, learning to draft patterns for "Meriken-style" dresses.
    
- **The Custom Shop:** Unlike the mass-production factories, these workers dealt with fine silks and wools, creating the tailored suits that "Schoolboys" wore in their deceptive photographs to send back to Japan.

### 4. Detailed Working Conditions

|**Feature**|**Description in 1920 Little Tokyo**|
|---|---|
|**The "Sweat" Factor**|Factories were poorly ventilated. In the L.A. summer, the heat from the steam irons and the friction of the machines made the air thick and stifling.|
|**Lighting**|Before widespread high-output electricity, women sat near windows to catch "north light," leading to severe eye strain by late afternoon.|
|**Noise**|The "cacophony of the treadle." In a room with 20 power machines, the vibration was so intense it could be felt through the floorboards of the neighboring shops.|
|**Wages**|A "good" worker might earn $12 to $18 a week, but after paying for thread, needle breakages (which some bosses deducted), and "room and board," the savings were minimal.|

### 5. Research Gaps and Biases

- **The "Hidden" Child Labor:** Archival records rarely mention children, but oral histories suggest that daughters as young as 10 often worked alongside their mothers in the "folding and trimming" phase. This was "invisible labor" that never appeared on a census.
    
- **The Racial Wage Gap:** Japanese women were often paid less than white or Jewish garment workers in the same city for the same piece-rate work, a fact often obscured by the "ethnic solidarity" narrative of Little Tokyo's business owners.
    
- **The "Health" Erasure:** The 1920s saw high rates of **tuberculosis** among garment workers due to the inhalation of fabric lint (_byssinosis_) in poorly ventilated SROs and basements. These health crises were often kept within the community to avoid giving "nativist" politicians an excuse to shut down Japanese businesses.
