"The Africans in the New World – African-Americans, but also the Antilleans, Brazilians, etc., escaped the abyss and carry within them the abyss’s dimension. The Africans had lost everything; they had nothing, not even a song. In jazz, Black Americans had to recompose through memory and extraordinary suffering, the echo of what Africa had meant for them. Jazz didn’t come from a book, but from a leak of painful memories. That’s why jazz is valid for everybody, not just for Black people, because it’s a reconstruction of a distraught memory of something that had disappeared and we are bringing back. It required a terrifying effort. That’s why jazz at the beginning was so tragic."

—Glissant, from _Edouard Glissant: One World in Relation_, dir. Manthia Diawara (2010) [[diawara-2010-documentary]]
