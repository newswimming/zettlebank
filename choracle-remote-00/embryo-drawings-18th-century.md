Contemporary scholars have noted that embryological images of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries rarely represent the types of composite objects reflecting the “truth to nature” that [[daston-and-galison-2007]] have found in eighteenth-century scientific images.

Instead they present wholly invented objects that the observers could not possibly have seen; this has led Clara Pinto-Correia, for instance, to speculate that one naturalist’s drawing of a fully formed fetus inside an ovary (rather than a uterus) must have been either “a symbolic representation” or “a gross misinterpretation” of actual embryonic development.
