Initially, a critique of television in the sixties, video was important because it was less burdened by a strictly formal definition of art that weighed down criticism during the postwar period in the US. From the beginning, video art subverted the language of mass media and its representational modes and strategies, which enabled the medium to break out of the confines of the traditional gallery space into wider communities. Inherent within video is a political desire to critique media representations, as a hybrid medium consisting of signals that prefigured the data-based logic of the Internet. The works discussed in this issue represent Asian diasporic artists remaking the problematic imagery of Asian subjects in North American mass media through their video-based practices, with race, ethnicity, gender, class, and sexuality as the loci of their reconfiguration.

Leading up to Crenshaw’s sharpened definition of intersectionality, Black feminists had been problematizing since the 1970s the combination of discriminations they faced in terms of race, gender, sexuality, and class, rather than each acting as isolated factors. The Combahee River Collective’s work is foundational in this effort, starting with “The Combahee River Collective Statement,” from 1977, which stated, “race, sex, and class are simultaneous factors in oppression,” and that, “[t]he major source of difficulty in our political work is that we are not just trying to fight oppression on one front or even two, but instead to address a whole range of oppressions.”

the distinctions between the Third World solidarity of the 1970s and 1980s and the intersectionality of the 1990s. In Woman, Native, Other (1989), Trinh T. Minh-ha synthesized feminism and Third World movements to critique Anglo-American neocolonialist patriarchy and its anthropological othering of women of colour, through her readings of writers such as Audrey Lorde, Leslie Marmon Silko, Gloria Anzaldúa, and Mitsuye Yamada. This work, contemporary to the findings of Crenshaw, saw the act of writing as elucidating the “complex relations of a subject caught between the problems of race and gender.”7 The distinction is that Trinh’s conceptualization of Third World women sees them as caught between the factors of race and gender, whereas Crenshaw’s intersectionality sees women of color’s subjectivities determined by the simultaneous factors of both race and gender, along with class. As intersectionality has grown in scope and importance as an analytical framework within feminism, it has proven to be flexible and multifaceted, able to incorporate other factors, such as sexuality and disability, into its critical framework.

Society for Early Transpacific Studies

The society was born with the idea of founding a community dedicated not to the investigation of a geographic region or territory, but to the study of the vast social space generated by the exchanges of the Manila Galleon.

Therefore, the term "Transpacific" is consistent with the definition of the Spanish Pacific. In other words, the Spanish Pacific of the early modern period is the space in Southeast Asia and the East that the Spanish Crown and its subjects imagined as a transpacific extension of the Spanish empire in the Americas, a region that included the Philippines and the Mariana Islands, where Spain effectively established itself as a colonial power, but also parts of China, Japan, and the Moluccas, which Spain mapped as part of the hemisphere assigned to it by the Treaty of Tordesillas of 1494.

This imagery was driven by the project of evangelization, which resulted in the imposition of Spanish moral codes and laws in officially conquered territories, such as the Philippines, and in varying levels of cultural and religious adaptation in impregnable areas such as China and Japan.
