In 1905, Los Angeles was the burgeoning heart of the Japanese immigrant community. For a "schoolboy"—often a young man of samurai or educated background—English Missions were the most vital social and educational hubs. These missions offered "Night Schools" where immigrants could study English, Western etiquette, and the Bible after a long day of domestic labor.

The following missions were the primary centers active in Los Angeles around **1905**:

### 1. The Japanese Methodist Episcopal Mission (The "Gospel Society")

This was the most influential and oldest Japanese Christian institution in the city. Founded in **1896** by a small group of _Issei_ (first-generation immigrants), it eventually became what is now the Century United Methodist Church.

- **The Schoolboy Experience:** It operated an English-language night school specifically designed to help students prepare for entrance into American high schools and universities.
    
- **Cultural Hub:** It provided a "Mission Home" or boarding house environment, offering a safe, alcohol-free social space that contrasted with the rougher gambling houses and saloons of early Little Tokyo.
    

### 2. The Japanese Christian Institute (Disciples of Christ)

Established in **1904** (just one year before your target date), this mission was sponsored by the Disciples of Christ.

- **Support Services:** Beyond English lessons, it focused heavily on the immediate survival needs of new arrivals, providing temporary housing and job placement services.
    
- **Evolution:** This mission would eventually grow into the All Peoples Christian Church, serving as a pillar of multiracial social justice in Los Angeles.
    

### 3. The Japanese Congregational Mission

While the major "Independent" Japanese Congregational Church was formally incorporated in 1906, mission work was active in 1905 under the supervision of the Home Missionary Society.

- **The Philosophy:** These missions often emphasized "self-support and self-governance," which appealed to the proud, former samurai "schoolboys" who wanted to maintain autonomy even while learning Western ways.
    
- **Location:** Much of this work centered around the East First Street area, which was rapidly becoming the commercial and spiritual core of Little Tokyo.
    

---

### Why "Schoolboys" Chose Missions

For a young man from a displaced elite family in Japan, these missions were more than religious centers; they were **social bridges**.

- **Credentialing:** Attending a mission school provided a "letter of recommendation" of sorts, signaling to white employers that the schoolboy was "moral," "temperate," and "civilized."
- **The "Gospel Society" Model:** These missions often mirrored the "Gospel Societies" (_Fukuin Kai_) founded in San Francisco, which functioned as a combination of a fraternity, a library, and a labor union.
- **English as Power:** In 1905, with anti-Japanese sentiment rising (the Asiatic Exclusion League was formed that very year), English fluency was the primary tool a schoolboy used to defend his rights and seek upward mobility.
