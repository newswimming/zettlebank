
In the early 1900s, a clerk’s daughter or a schoolboy in Japan wouldn't just have one name for America. Depending on whether they were writing a formal letter, reading a newspaper, or gossiping at a port, they used different terms that reflected the era's unique linguistic blend.

## 1. _Meriken_ (The Vernacular Sound)

In daily speech, particularly among those living in port cities or planning to emigrate, America was often called **"Meriken" (メリケン)**.

- **The Etymology:** This was a phonetic "mishearing" of the word **"American."** Early Japanese speakers often dropped the initial unstressed "A," resulting in "Meriken."
    
- **Cultural Artifacts:** Even today, you can find remnants of this in Japan:
    
    - **Meriken-ko:** Western-style wheat flour (as opposed to traditional rice flour).
        
    - **Meriken Hatoba:** "American Wharf," a common name for docks where foreign ships landed (most famously in Kobe).
        
    - **Meriken-bari:** Western-style sewing needles.
        

## 2. _Beikoku_ (The "Rice Country")

If your clerk’s daughter was reading a newspaper or official documents, she would see America referred to as **Beikoku (米国)**.

- **The Kanji Logic:** To write "America" phonetically in kanji (_ateji_), the Meiji-era scholars used **亜米利加 (A-me-ri-ka)**.
    
- **The Abbreviation:** To keep headlines short, they used only one character to represent the whole country. Since the first character **"亜" (A)** was already being used for **Asia (亜細亜)**, they took the second character **"米" (me/bei)**, which means **rice**.
    
- **The Irony:** Consequently, the written name for America literally translates to **"Rice Country,"** despite it not being a rice-focused culture.
    

## 3. _Shin-Sekai_ (The "New World")

For those with a more poetic or academic background, America was sometimes referred to as the **Shin-Sekai (新世界)**.

- **The Allure:** This term carried the weight of opportunity and reinvention. To an educated girl facing a stagnant future in a Japanese village, the "New World" promised a departure from the rigid "Good Wife, Wise Mother" expectations of the Meiji government.

### Research Transparency: A Note on the "A"

While the transition from _Meriken_ to _Amerika_ (with the full initial "A") was happening in the 1910s and 20s, archival audio and diaries suggest the shift was generational. Younger "Schoolboys" were more likely to use the full "Amerika" to show off their English proficiency, while their parents back in the village might still say _Meriken_ or simply refer to it as **"The Other Side" (_Mukō_)**.


## 1. _Shoninko_ (The "Little Tokyo" Translation)

The most common formal name used within the community was **Shoninko (小東京)**.

- **The Etymology:** This is a literal translation. _Sho_ (小) means small, and _Ninko_ is a phonetic shortening of "Tokyo."
    
- **Usage:** You would see this on the masthead of newspapers like the _Rafu Shimpo_ or in the names of community organizations. It acknowledged the neighborhood as a "miniature" version of the Japanese capital.
    

## 2. _Rafu_ (The "L.A." Name)

Perhaps the most ubiquitous term used by residents was **Rafu (羅府)**.

- **The Logic:** In the 1920s, Japanese immigrants used _ateji_ (kanji used for phonetics) to name American cities. Los Angeles was written as **羅府 (Ra-fu)**.
    
    - _Ra_ (羅) was taken from the middle of "Los Angeles" (specifically the "la" sound).
        
    - _Fu_ (府) means "prefectural capital" or "government office."
        
- **Usage:** Residents wouldn't say they lived in "Little Tokyo"; they would say they lived in **Rafu**. Even today, the city's main Japanese newspaper is the _Rafu Shimpo_ (The Los Angeles News).
    

## 3. _Nihonmachi_ (The "Japantown" Standard)

When speaking generally about the ethnic enclave, residents used the term **Nihonmachi (日本町)**.

- **The Meaning:** Literally "Japanese Town."
    
- **The Context:** This was a pan-Pacific term. Whether you were in San Francisco, Seattle, or Los Angeles, the Japanese district was always the _Nihonmachi_. It emphasized the cultural boundaries of the neighborhood—a place where you could speak Japanese, eat traditional food, and escape the "Meriken" world outside.

## 4. Specific Sub-District Names

Within the neighborhood, residents used more granular terms to describe specific zones:

- **First Street (_Ichiban-gai_):** The heart of the commercial district was often simply called _Ichiban-gai_. This was the "Main Street" where the prestigious shops and professional offices were located.
    
- **Weller Street Area:** As we discussed with the Umeya factory, the streets south of First Street were seen as the more "industrial" or "working-class" sections, often associated with boarding houses and bachelor laborers.

## 5. Transparency: Bias and Class in Naming

As a research assistant, I should note that the choice of name often signaled a resident's social aspirations:

- **The "Respectable" Class:** Merchants and community leaders preferred **Shoninko**, as it sounded official and equated Los Angeles with a world-class city like Tokyo.
    
- **The Working Class:** Laborers and "Schoolboys" more frequently used **Rafu**, treating it as a localized, immigrant-only slang that differentiated their experience from both Japan and white America.
    
- **The White Perspective:** In 1920s Los Angeles city records and white-owned newspapers (like the _L.A. Times_), the area was often pejoratively called **"Japtown."** Japanese residents almost never used this term themselves, as they were acutely aware of the racist connotations.
